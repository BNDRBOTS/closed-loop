# The Closed Loop: How Industry-Funded Research Manufactures Platform Dependency in Nonprofit Fundraising

---

## The Mechanism

The dominant "industry research" informing how nonprofits choose fundraising platforms is produced by, funded by, or structurally entangled with the platforms themselves. In every documented case, the conclusions route back to the funders' paid products. The loop runs through five distinct nodes.

---

## Node 1: Blackbaud Institute → Blackbaud Products

**The entity:** The Blackbaud Institute is explicitly "a research lab at Blackbaud (NASDAQ: BLKB), the leading provider of software for powering social impact."

**The data source:** Its annual *Trends in Giving* reports draw exclusively from "Blackbaud's proprietary philanthropic dataset" — giving data pulled from Blackbaud's own customer base of over 7,500 nonprofit organizations.

**The conclusions:** The 2025 *Trends in Giving Spotlight* recommends nonprofits prioritize AI-optimized online donation forms, Agentic AI tools for midlevel donor engagement, and online giving platform investment. These are Blackbaud's own product lines.

**The loop:**
> Blackbaud's internal research lab → Uses Blackbaud's customer data → Produces "industry benchmarks" → Recommends Blackbaud's tools → Distributed free as a "commitment to social impact"

The vendor's lab, the vendor's data, and the vendor's recommendations form a closed circuit distributed as neutral research.

*Sources: Blackbaud newsroom, March 18, 2026; PR Newswire, March 20, 2025*

---

## Node 2: Classy / GoFundMe → "State of Modern Philanthropy"

**The entity:** Classy was acquired by GoFundMe and rebranded as GoFundMe Pro. Its flagship annual report, *The State of Modern Philanthropy*, "combines Classy platform data with insights from our parent company, GoFundMe." Their combined user community is cited at over 150 million donors.

**The conclusions (2023 edition):**
- Embedded donation forms produce 2x industry-average conversion rates
- Recurring donors are 9x more valuable than one-time donors
- Peer-to-peer campaigns acquire 80% new donors to nonprofits
- Supporters participating in multiple campaign types are 4x more valuable

Every major finding maps directly to Classy/GoFundMe Pro's paid feature set.

**The explicit sales-script function:** Classy's own report preview page states the material was built so representatives could "speak confidently about Classy even when addressing some of the most common challenges and concerns nonprofits have about adopting a new online fundraising platform." The research is a sales objection-handling tool — stated in their own words.

**The loop:**
> GoFundMe funds report → Report uses GoFundMe/Classy platform data → Report recommends GoFundMe/Classy features → Sales team uses report to overcome switching objections → Nonprofits pay platform fees → Funds next report

*Sources: Classy.org / GoFundMe Pro report landing page; NonprofitPro, April 2024; GoFundMe Pro sneak-peek page (archived)*

---

## Node 3: BBB Give.org → Charity Seal Sales

**The entity:** BBB Wise Giving Alliance (BBB's Give.org) is a "standards-based charity evaluator" that issues certifications verifying charities against its own 20 Standards for Charity Accountability. Certification is its product.

**The research it produces:** The annual *Donor Trust Report* tracks donor trust across charity categories and giving channels. A June 2026 special study — *Online Giving Platforms and Donor Expectations* — found that 62% of online giving platform users say a charity's presence on a well-known platform increases their trust in that charity.

**The loop's internal structure:**
> Give.org produces data showing "donors trust charities on verified platforms" → Give.org is a platform verification provider → Same report directs donors to "visit Give.org to verify if a charity meets the 20 BBB Standards for Charity Accountability" → Give.org sells the verification product the data says donors require

The entity producing the "trust is essential and tied to platform presence" narrative also sells the trust mechanism.

**The secondary amplification loop:** Once produced, Give.org's trust framing gets cited by major platforms as independent evidence that independent or unknown giving channels face donor skepticism — which reinforces the case for using established, fee-bearing platform infrastructure. The same data point circulates in fundraising consulting, nonprofit CMS vendor content, and payment processor guides as settled fact.

*Sources: BBB Give.org Special Donor Trust Report, June 16, 2026; Give.org Donor Trust Report 2026, May 5, 2026*

---

## Node 4: The Academic Layer — Giving USA / The Giving Institute

**The apparent counterpoint:** Giving USA — the longest-running, most widely cited annual report on U.S. charitable giving — is researched and written by the Indiana University Lilly Family School of Philanthropy. It carries genuine academic methodology and peer-reviewed credibility. It is the closest thing to a neutral benchmark the sector has.

**The structural entanglement:**

Giving USA is published by the *Giving USA Foundation*, described officially as "a public service initiative of The Giving Institute." The Giving Institute is a trade association of fundraising consulting firms. Its member organizations — firms that consult nonprofits on fundraising strategy, often recommending institutional platforms — provide "expertise and volunteer leadership" to the Foundation that publishes the report the sector treats as gospel.

**Sponsored research at the academic level:**

The Lilly School itself conducts commissioned research with financial and fundraising industry partners:
- The *Study of High Net Worth Philanthropy* (biennial) runs in formal partnership with U.S. Trust
- The planned-giving donor behavior study runs in partnership with Pentera, a planned-giving consulting firm
- The *Giving in Chicago* report was produced in conjunction with the Chicago Community Trust

The school explicitly offers contract research: "Contract with us to examine philanthropic behaviors and motivations."

**The implication:**

This does not invalidate the Lilly School's methodology. What it demonstrates is that industry money is woven into the research infrastructure at every level — including the academic tier — not only at the vendor or evaluator tier. The benchmark the entire sector cites sits inside a foundation run by a trade association of the industry's own consultants.

*Sources: Giving USA 2025 release, IU Lilly Family School of Philanthropy; Lilly School Ongoing Research page; Encyclopedia of Indianapolis entry on Lilly Family School; Lilly School Research Funding page*

## Node 5: Vendor Content Marketing — The Retail Layer

This tier operates below the institutional level but reinforces the same loop at high volume and high search visibility.

**Morweb** (nonprofit CMS vendor) publishes *The Nonprofit Website Trust Checklist: 27 Things Donors Check Before Giving*, establishing a multi-signal trust standard, then offers to "run your site against the full 27-signal checklist and send a prioritized report" — a direct conversion funnel from trust-standard content to paid consulting and CMS adoption.

**WildApricot** publishes *The 17 Best Online Payment Processors for Nonprofits*, a guide emphasizing PCI compliance, established brand recognition, and platform trust signals. It is distributed as independent guidance while serving as a product comparison page for WildApricot's own integrated payment tools.

**The loop:**
> Vendor publishes trust standard → Standard implies compliance requirement → Vendor sells compliance solution → Content ranks in search → Nonprofits discover trust requirement and vendor simultaneously

*Sources: Morweb.org, May 2026; WildApricot.com, March 2026*

## What the Actual Donor Behavioral Data Shows

The "donor trust" narrative, as distributed through the entities above, implies donors are skeptical of or unwilling to engage with unfamiliar or independent giving channels. The behavioral data from the same studies directly contradicts this framing.

From the BBB Give.org *Donor Trust Report 2026* (May 2026) and the *Special Donor Trust Report on Online Giving Platforms* (June 2026):

- **42.1% of U.S. adults** are open to being approached by charities — one of the highest levels recorded in nine years of tracking
- **54% of online giving platform users** discover charities through social media; **40% discover through friends**
- **82% of Gen Z and Millennials** actively use giving platforms specifically to discover new charities
- Trust in charities increased across **12 of 13 charity categories** between 2024 and 2025
- Younger donors show dramatically higher openness: **63% of Gen Z** say they might give more if asked

This data describes a giving environment where behavior is driven by peer and social signals — the precise mechanism underlying Venmo, Cash App, and direct peer-to-peer campaigns. It is not a picture of a donor base that requires institutional verification or platform presence before acting. The data these entities produce undermines the conclusions those same entities draw from it.

---

## The Defensible Thesis

**What the evidence holds:**

Industry money is embedded throughout the research infrastructure describing how donors behave and what nonprofits need — from vendor-operated research labs (Blackbaud Institute) to parent-platform-funded annual reports (Classy/GoFundMe) to seal-selling evaluators producing "trust is tied to platform presence" data (BBB Give.org) to academic research carrying financial industry sponsorship (IU Lilly School). In every case, the research conclusions route toward the funders' paid products or certification systems.

**What the evidence does not support:**

The claim that a "trust barrier" prevents donors from engaging with independent or peer-organized giving channels is not supported by the behavioral data — including the data produced by the same entities making the claim. Donors discover and act through social and peer channels at high rates, particularly among younger demographics. The barrier being manufactured is not fear of unknown links. It is organizational dependency on platform infrastructure — fee-bearing, data-extracting, tip-prompting platform infrastructure.

**The precise claim this evidence supports:**

These research systems do not primarily describe donor behavior. They prescribe platform dependency. The data is real. The framing around it is constructed to route nonprofits into fee-bearing platform relationships by overstating the role of institutional trust signals and systematically understating the role of peer-to-peer giving channels — channels that require none of those signals to function and that the behavioral data confirms are how giving actually moves.
